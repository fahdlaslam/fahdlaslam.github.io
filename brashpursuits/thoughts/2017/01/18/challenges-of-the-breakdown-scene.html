Challenges of the Breakdown Scene

A few nights ago, I shot the most intense scene of my career so far. I won’t talk about the project or any specifics about my performance, because it’s still fresh in my mind, and of course, these things always take time to process, but I wanted to note out a few of the specific challenges of what every actor fears and loves most: 

<< THE BREAKDOWN SCENE >>

The breakdown scene is, of course, that scene where everything goes to shit and we get all the catharsis and emotional reeling we’ve probably been watching whatever we’re watching to see in the first place.

But, thinking about the breakdown scene in this way can damage your performance. It’s important to realize that this scene, like any other, is just a scene. Giving it tantamount importance based on its emotional volatility is a trap, simple as that.

So what are some things to think about to avoid giving the breakdown scene power over you, instead of you having power over it?

1. Be Willing To Look Stupid. This is probably the number one thing I struggle most with as an actor, and it rears its ugly head here more than anywhere else. No matter how seriously charged the scene is, you have to be completely willing to get it wrong, or you probably won’t go as far as you have to go.

2. Be Willing To Go All The Way. Thanks to the newer schools of thought around acting, more and more actors (myself included) are afraid of “overacting.” Listen, I’m not advocating that you should ham it up. We all know how bad it looks when you… Well, act badly. But that’s something you have to set aside for scenes like this one. Your character is out of control, and while I would never advocate an actor completely lose control of what’s going on, you can afford to loosen the reins a little.

3. Trust The People Around You. Your director is seeing it. Your cinematographer is seeing it. The grips are seeing it. Everybody is seeing it. Except you. So do whatever is asked of you (within reason) and trust that it’s going to turn out. It’s a particular challenge for me not to direct myself. But I’m not Ben Affleck. 

4. Physicalize. Movement is likely my weakest area as an actor. That’s a whole ‘nother post, but I will say that if you’re failing yourself in a big, emotional scene (or any scene, really) the easiest way to get out of the trap is to do something different with your body. Even if all the blocking you’ve done on camera is set in stone because you’re 15 takes in, you can still do something before they start rolling to shake yourself up. And you should. Just watch literally any footage of Jack Nicholson playing like, literally any character. If he didn’t look like an idiot, you probably won’t either. And even if you do, they’ll be thanking you when the final cut is getting sent out to festivals.

There are many other takeaways I got from this work, but since it was my first really aggressively emotional scene, I don’t want to make the list much longer, because look, I’m not an expert. I don’t think anybody really is. Sometimes a non-actor will nail a really emotional scene without any pressing, and sometimes seasoned professionals will really struggle with them. It’s the nature of the game. 

If you have any hard-edged advice on how to cut an emotional scene down to size, shoot me an email at tim@brashpursuits.com! I’d love to hear from you!
