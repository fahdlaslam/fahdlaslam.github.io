Nobody Knows What We Do

I was hit by this idea the other day. Actually, it might be closer to the truth to say it’s been slowly hitting me since I started studying acting, and the other day I was just able to pin a phrase to it and say “This is what I’ve been feeling.”

Nobody knows what we do.

It’s something that I think everyone tries to ignore. Whole systems have been built to tell you that someone does know what we do. Hundreds and thousands of dollars get passed from student to teacher to figure out what we do. I’ve had many a director (god bless them all) ask me what will help me, what will make me feel more comfortable, what will get me to do what they’re looking for. Some of them come into the first rehearsal with buckets full of ideas that they’ve picked up secondhand from other actors, or teachers at universities, on how to get me to do what they want. 

Ask any actor what their process is, and you’ll get a lot of information. But you’ll get more information in that moment when their eyes glaze over, and they run into a part of the process that they can’t really explain. I’ve often had to justify, to myself and others, a weird step I took in the development of a role. I didn’t know why I was doing it. I didn’t know what use it was. At the end of the day, I couldn’t say if it was even useful at all. But I felt some subterranean urge to do it. I just didn’t know why.

The onus is on us, in this industry, to constantly appear to know what we’re doing. When you walk into an audition, there’s a certain sense that you need to look like you know what you’re doing. On a certain level, you absolutely do. But I think on another, being able to have no idea what you’re doing, and still do it fully anyway, is the actor’s biggest gift to the audience. 

After all, the audience doesn’t want to know what we’re doing. We celebrate actors as the foremost artists in modern society, prying into their personal lives more than even politicians. But at the end of the day, we don’t actually know or care what they’re doing on stage or screen. We only care what their character appears to be doing. 

And how do we create that behavior, that essence, that life?

I don’t know.
