The Authentic Actor By Michael Laskin

Acting books. We’ve all read one. Every acting class I’ve ever been in has assigned at least one, whether it was a classic source like An Actor Prepares or something written by the instructor themselves. 

At this point, I must’ve read twenty or thirty books on acting or more. A lot of them repeat and rehash the same old tried and true approach to acting. The spine of a role, actions, beats, et cetera et cetera ad nauseam. 

My favorites though, tend to offer a vastly different approach to acting. They certainly build from the foundations built by Stanislavski and the Group Theater, but they certainly don’t stop there. They take into account the day-by-day advancements of the impossible art form.

Michael Laskin’s book The Authentic Actor is just such an entry into the score of acting literature. In it, Laskin gleefully tosses out ideas and philosophies that, while complementary of the foundational work of the actor, also advocate for a shift in the paradigm. There’s no point, really, in doing all of the homework if it doesn’t result in real, living performance. 

Laskin’s language is so his own that it would be a crime to try and sum the book up here. Really, you can look at the title and know what the book is more or less “about.” But while the book’s thesis is incredibly important, what’s more important is the way Laskin leads you to its conclusion. There’s wit, insight, and a nurturing sense of assurance here. It is one of the only times I’ve ever felt like a book was particularly kind to me, and I don’t mean that it gave me a pat on the back and a gold star just for showing up.

Laskin is honest about the harsh realities of the acting world, but he dispenses equally honest advice on how to navigate that harshness. It isn’t easy to show up and be completely yourself, and it also isn’t easy to write a book on how to do that.

But Michael Laskin succeeds. I won’t spoil how or why, because I think you simply have to read the book for yourself, but I can assure you that it’s one that belongs on the most perused part of your shelf, to be revisited in times of calm and times of strife. Most spiritual and philosophical acting texts, in their divorce from the pure study of technique, can go far out in their search for a new definition of acting. But The Authentic Actor sticks with the definition of acting we’ve had since the days of Brando and James Dean, and simply adds the years since, and the insights about what acting is that have come along with them, onto what we already knew.

For the actor, most roads of training lead you to do something as opposed to being what you are. Michael Laskin’s book is a necessary look in a different direction, and a joyful one at that.
