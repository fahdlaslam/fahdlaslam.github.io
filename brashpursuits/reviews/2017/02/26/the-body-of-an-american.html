The Body of an American

I have been trying to frame my experience with The Body of an American since I saw it a few nights ago. I could certainly describe the production from the standard perspective, with questions like “Was the staging interesting?” or “Was the acting good?” Given the subject matter of the play, I could even take the political approach and talk about the themes the play speaks to. It is a play about a war photographer, so it is rife with political thoughts and positions. 

But covering the standard ground of theatre criticism isn’t particularly interesting to me in the case of this play. For one, I’m not really qualified to say whether this or that element was good or bad. I never see more than five or ten plays a year, so who am I to really say if the play succeeds on this or that level?

What I can say is that the play haunted me in much the same way Paul Watson’s Fresh Air interview haunted Dan O’Brien. When I saw the two in person at a talk at Primary Stages, Paul Watson remarked how unlikely it is that the events of his life would line up in such a way that this play was written, and then put on, and that he would end up in that room talking about those things at that moment. 

This unlikeliness, this existential question, this anxiety about the determination of all things, is what grips me about the play. It’s what keeps me thinking about it, even when I don’t want to. 

The play is not always a comfortable space to be in. It is shockingly entertaining given its subject matter, but the malady that these two men share, frankly and earnestly, made it very, very difficult for me to contend with. But I think it was important that I did. For possibly the first time ever in the theater, I was able to hear two people ask the questions that I know I’ll never get an answer to. In drama, we routinely have characters who take action, who do not stop to really reflect on what this life actually is. I think this is largely because if the characters don’t take action, and do stop to have long, meandering conversations about “the nature of things” the play becomes boring, navel-gazey, and self-indulgent.

But somehow, The Body Of An American doesn’t do that. The play manages to move at times very slowly through a very claustrophobic psychological space, and this makes it horrifying and wonderful instead of tiresome and boring. Perhaps it’s just because everything in the play is true. I really don’t know.

And I’m certain that most people’s experience of the play will probably not reflect mine. Most people are more interested in politics than me, most people are more socially conscious and aware of world events. Most people will be able to look at it is “an intriguing play.” 

I, however, will always remember it as two actors performing a ghost story wherein, somehow, I am one of the ghosts.

And this is what really moves me about it. This is the kind of thing I hope I’m able to do one day. Plays like this are the reason plays are made. I can’t say any more about it because I very frankly don’t know how, but I think, in fact, that is actually a very good thing.

The Body Of An American continues its run at The Cherry Lane Theater from now until March 20th. For more information about the play, and more details about all the brilliant people involved, please visit http://primarystages.org/shows/current-season/the-body-of-an-american.
